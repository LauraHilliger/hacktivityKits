A Hack Jam is a great way to team up people with different skill-sets to explore an interesting challenge, collaboratively build something new or improve something that already exists, learn and teach new hacking skills. Replace this description with your own!

Before your Event

Find a partner

Who do you know that might want to help you organize this hackjam? Are there other Mozillians in your area?

Find a Venue

Reserve a comfortable venue where you can move the chairs and sit together in a circle. Be creative—maybe try a cafe, library or a hackerspace? Ensure there is Internet, power and good lighting. And don't forget snacks!

Who will you invite? How will you promote?

Invite people with diverse skills. Talk to participants beforehand about what they're interested in making and what motivates them. Share your planning process with the Webmaker Community, where you can ask for help and feedback.

Find an Agenda

Find or remix a teaching kit to use as the base of your agenda, or make your own from the beginning!

During your Event

Get to know your participants

How will you welcome the group and invite them to introduce themselves? Will you can do a quick go-around in a circle and afterward try this fun icebreaker, the Spectrogram?

  • Recommended time: 20 minutes
  • Discuss short, controversial statements that helps introduce participants and the event's themes.

Set Up

What do participants need to set up? List it here!
  • Persona
  • Webmaker Account
  • Badge Backpack

Run through your Agenda

  • Allow participants to hack your agenda
  • Cluster ideas and build teams
  • Hack in Small Groups: Form small groups based on the participants' chosen projects. Adjust group sizes as needed; 5 people or less is best. Float around to make sure each group is on track.
  • Make Something: Prompt the groups to draw, write and code as much as they can. Don't let tech troubles distract you. Paper is just as useful for prototyping as code, and drawing is just as fun.

Share and Celebrate!

Ask the groups to wrap up and prepare the room for presentations. Invite each group to share what they made. Reflect on the event, the projects and what everyone would like to do next.

Publish notes about the event where participants can find it, and add to it, after the event. Then do something fun together! Grab a drink or something to eat together. Go bowling. Take a silly photo and share it with #makerparty.

After your Event

Share with the World!

Collect the things you've made and write a quick blog post about your event. You can hack this guide to document your event by adding photos, text and links. Once you've documented everything, share your story with the Webmaker community.

Plan your next event

Send a follow up email to your participants and ask them what they thought of your event. You might also ask if they would like to collaborate on another event.
