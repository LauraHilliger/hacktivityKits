Activity

Roadmapping my next steps

Learners explore and discuss the next step for their ideas. Use this activity to help learners reflect on brainstorming activities or paper prototypes and create roadmaps to take their work into the future.

Made by Laura with thanks to Kat for this super activity and materials!

    Time to reflect

  1. A good way to start this activity is with a bit of thoughtful reflection before hands-on making. Talk about the ideas that have surfaced, and tell learners that a roadmap describes their idea and plans how it might become sustainable (e.g. make money, become known, attract contributors).

    Be sure to discuss that:

    • Roadmapping can function like a goal sheet or a business plan.
    • Roadmapping helps entrepreneurs think about risks, costs and opportunities
    • It allows them to document next steps so they know how to move forward
    • It shows the thoughtfulness of the idea
    • It helps them formulate their ideas so that others can understand.

    Making the Roadmaps

  2. Once it feels like participants are ready to draft their roadmaps, start handing out the Roadmapping My Next Steps template (PDF) (provide a few of these per kit) to each individual or group. Offer participants at least 30 mins to dig in, and ensure everyone is getting their hands dirty!

    Curating and Sharing

  3. If you have extra time, facilitate this Speed Geek Playtesting activity to guide participants through the process of sharing their roadmaps and getting feedback. Congratulate everyone on a job well done -- they have now officially created their next steps roadmap!

    This is also a good moment to suggest opportunities for participants to receive the mentoring and resources they need to take their next steps with a global community. Encourage them to continue to work together, and connect online with the Teach the Web community. Come share ideas on our weekly Teach the Web community call.

Materials

  • Template: Roadmapping my next steps (PDF version)
  • Pens and fun markers
  • Post-its -- lots of them!
  • Recycled printing paper

Facilitator's Notes

Don't forget to decide whether you want participants to build out their ideas alone or together as co-designers. Groupwork focus? Try starting by defining a problem with an activity this Problem Defining Mindmap to help participants organize themselves around issues they want to solve. Discuss why goal setting and connecting to other people is important. The facilitator should use this activity to help determine what learners want to do with their ideas after a workshop, and help them explore the benefits of connection in a guided discussion.
