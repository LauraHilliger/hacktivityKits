Workshop Title

Template for 4 Hour Agenda

[25 mins] Introductions, Persona and Profiling

Start by introducing yourself and explaining what YOU want them to learn. Include links to the workshop etherpad and explain that you're keeping a pad to stay connected, catalog links and share work. Ask questions to get an idea of who's there and peers that can pair up to help one another.

  • Who can code?
  • Who can teach?
  • Who has experience with Webmaking tools specifically?

Introductions

To keep introductions short, use a prompt that allows people to say a single unique thing. For example, "My name is, I work on/for/at, and if you really knew me, you'd know..."

A Facilitator has to be willing to make a fool out of him/herself, so choose a game to loosen people up and be animated in the way you begin. People are self conscious, show them that you see everyone as 100% awesome. Try to remain positive the entire time. When you have "problem people" be firm, but nice, and remember you're the workshop leader.

Persona and the Badge Backback

Get everyone to set up Persona and their Badge Backpack

Profiling

Have participants draw themselves and label the drawing with one word that they self identify as and their names. Add other information about themselves while eating.

This exercise helps facilitators make associations between words, names and faces. Plus it helps you judge the personality-type that people have. If people feel like you're trying to know them, they LET you teach. EVERYONE likes hands on exercises ;)

[30 mins] ***FOOD***

Feed people. Or at least give them coffee. It's good to give people a topic to talk about while they're eating.

[15 mins] Icebreaker Activity

Use a hacktivity (like the Spectrogram) that introduces the topic for the day. This puts participants in a particular mind frame and prepares them for learning.

  • Anyone who writes in this day and age, is writing for the web.
  • It's important that everyone learn basic HTML and CSS.
  • This is another set of tools to do excellent journalism.

[10 mins] Break into Groups

No one has to work alone. They will work independently, but with peers. Oh, sounds impossible? No, participants get to ask the person next to them for help instead of raising your hand. This isn't elementary school! Explain that you'll be floating and help people that get stuck. Tell people to work with people they don't know!

[30 mins] MAKE

A good second activity is one that gives pointed instruction to participants on tools or procedures. An example would be allowing the participants to get used to the interface of Thimble or Popcorn by having them make projects. Before learners can practically apply new skills, they need to be comfortable with the tools they'll be using and have a basic understanding of the skills they'll be sharpening.

You float. Be sure to point people to additional resources, websites or cheatsheets!

[20 mins] Who are we and why are we working together? (+Q&A)

Recircle and give people context about you or your organization. Never do this at the beginning of a session, but do it. By now they're interested to know where you came from (if you're doing your job right ;)

[1hr 20 mins] MAKE

Everyone forms groups and collaborate to do a third activity. A practical, project based activity allows learners to delve deeper into the essential questions of your session.

Break into small groups
  • In small teams, start brainstorming about the challenge and write down as many solution as possible. Rough notes and sketches are fine!
  • Regroup for a few minutes and present the best ideas. Try a Speedgeek format!
Start Hacking
  • Go back into teams and focus on the ideas that got the most traction.
  • Start drafting the prototypes on paper. This helps formulate ideas and exposes any holes.
  • Consider design elements to simplify and improve the user's experience.
Sprint to Finish
  • Keep refining the paper prototype or using other techniques and tools, like Thimble, to get closer to a workable solution.
  • Teams sprint to finish their "minimum viable prototype."
  • Each team prepares a short demo or presentation of their project.

[30 min] Closing Circle

Use the closing circle to celebrate what you made. Have participants present their prototypes and encourage them to think about what they want to do next with their project.

If you like, share your prototypes with the world! You can publish the links and other materials to a blog or the social network of your choice.
